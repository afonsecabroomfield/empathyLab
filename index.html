Introduction to Web Accessibility

When websites and web tools are properly designed and coded, people with disabilities can use them. However, currently many sites and tools are developed with accessibility barriers that make them difficult or impossible for some people to use. Making the web accessible benefits individuals, businesses, and society. International web standards define what is needed for accessibility.

What is Web Accessibility?

Web accessibility means that websites, tools, and technologies are designed and developed so that people with disabilities can use them. More specifically, people can: perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with the Web contribute to the Web Web accessibility encompasses all disabilities that affect access to the Web, including: auditory cognitive neurological physical speech visual

Benefits of Accessibility

Web accessibility also benefits people without disabilities, for example: people using mobile phones, smart watches, smart TVs, and other devices with small screens, different input modes, etc. older people with changing abilities due to ageing people with “temporary disabilities” such as a broken arm or lost glasses people with “situational limitations” such as in bright sunlight or in an environment where they cannot listen to audio people using a slow Internet connection, or who have limited or expensive bandwidth

Web accessibility is not a new concept, but it is now more relevant than ever, with almost 2 billion people living with disabilities worldwide and relying on the web for information, education, and more.

Imagine a table on a webpage as a neatly organized grid of information, like rows and columns in a spreadsheet. For someone relying on a screen reader due to visual impairment, this tool reads out the content, describing headers at the top that act like titles for each column. It enables easy navigation, allowing users to move through the table's cells and explore the information. Just as buildings have ramps for accessibility, web developers ensure tables are accessible to all by using features that work with screen readers, creating a welcoming online experience where everyone, regardless of abilities, can understand and engage with the content effortlessly.

Assistive technology refers to devices, tools, software, or equipment designed to help people with disabilities perform tasks that might be challenging or impossible for them otherwise. These technologies aim to enhance the independence, quality of life, and accessibility of individuals with disabilities. Assistive technology can cover a wide range of areas, including mobility, communication, hearing, vision, and cognitive abilities. Examples of assistive technology include screen readers for the visually impaired, hearing aids for those with hearing loss, communication devices for individuals with speech impairments, adaptive keyboards for those with motor challenges, and many other specialized tools tailored to meet specific needs. The goal of assistive technology is to bridge the gap between people with disabilities and the opportunities provided by technology, education, employment, and daily life.

  • Education and Awareness: All Employees should foster awareness and understanding of accessibility principles. Provide training sessions or resources on creating inclusive content, designing accessible interfaces, and considering diverse user needs.
  • Inclusive Communication: All Employees should use clear and concise language in written and verbal communication. Provide alternative formats for important information, such as meeting agendas, to accommodate diverse needs.
  • Document Accessibility: Content Creators should ensure that documents, presentations, and other content are created with accessibility in mind. This includes using proper heading structures, alternative text for images, and accessible document formats.
  • Continuous Improvement: Leadership and Management should foster a culture of continuous improvement by regularly assessing and enhancing accessibility policies, practices, and technologies within the organization.
  • Advocacy and Support: Managers and Colleagues should advocate for accessibility initiatives and support colleagues who may require accommodations. Foster an inclusive work environment where diversity and accessibility are valued.

Remember, promoting accessibility is an ongoing effort that involves collaboration across all levels of an organization. By working together, employees can contribute to creating a workplace that is inclusive and accommodating for everyone.
